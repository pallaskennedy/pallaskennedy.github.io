MAIN (framework) : The “living room” of the page, which contains the most important content. • The main content area where the primary focus of the page is displayed, such as articles, forms, or other interactive content.
SECTION (structure): The “room dividers” inside the main content. • Organizes content into distinct sections with related information. For example, on a blog page, you could have different sections for the blog post, related articles, and comments. Purpose: Used to group related content thematically. A section typically contains a heading and represents a standalone portion of content that is thematically related. • Usage: Use a section when you want to create distinct parts of the content that share a common theme or subject matter. • Example: In a blog post, you might have a section for the introduction, another for the main content, and a third for the conclusion. Use section,article and p for organizing textual content with meaning and context, much like paragraphs in an essay.
ARTICLE (structure): A “self-contained room” or “apartment” for individual content items. • Used for standalone content, like blog posts, news articles, or user-submitted content. Purpose: Represents a self-contained piece of content that could stand alone, such as a blog post, news article, or user-submitted content. It usually includes its own title and is meant to be distributable or syndicateable. • Usage: Use an article when the content is independently meaningful and could be published on its own, or when it makes sense to reference it outside of the surrounding context. • Example: Each blog post on a blog page can be wrapped in an article.
• Aside: The “sidebar” or “storage room” for secondary information. • Holds supplementary content related to the main content but not part of it, such as ads, additional links, or author bio information.
DIV (structure): • The “generic room” or “flex space.” • A versatile container that is used to group other elements together, often for layout purposes or styling. It doesn't carry any semantic meaning by itself but helps with structure. Purpose: A generic container used for grouping elements without any semantic meaning. It's primarily used for styling and layout purposes. • Usage: Use a div when there is no suitable semantic element to represent the content. It's helpful for applying CSS styles or JavaScript functionality but does not provide meaning to the content itself. • Example: Wrapping multiple sections together for styling or organizational purposes. Use div for grouping visual components or layout elements that don't have specific semantic meaning, much like organizing different parts of a drawing or design.
FORM (component) : • The “office” or “workroom” where users interact. • Used for input, such as user registrations, surveys, or logins.
FIELDSET (structure) : You typically use a fieldset instead of a section when you are grouping related form elements. The fieldset element is specifically designed for forms to group inputs, making it clear that they belong to a particular section of the form. It also allows you to optionally use a legend element to label the group.
Here's a comparison:
When to Use fieldset
• Group related form elements together (e.g., personal details, payment information).
• Provide a visual boundary around the group of form inputs.
• Use legend to give a label for the group.

When to Use section
• section is used for grouping content that is thematically related on the page, but not specifically for forms.
• It organizes large sections of the page, not just form elements.
• fieldset: Specifically for forms, helps group related inputs with an optional label (legend).
• section: Used for broader page content, more general for organizing non-form content.
